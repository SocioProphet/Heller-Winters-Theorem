## Editorial Notice

This manuscript is a cleaned, combined working version of the Heller–Winters material. It is intentionally conservative. It does **not** claim an unconditional proof of the Riemann Hypothesis, a closed-form next-prime formula, or a final theorem in the journal-proof sense. It states the Heller–Winters construction as a governed, falsifiable, reproducible program for organizing prime-distribution structure across deterministic constraints, log-scale phase analysis, quotient normalization, identity-channel separation, and evidence-ledger validation.

The word **theorem** is retained as the repository name and as the project’s target form. In this version, the rigorous object is the **Heller–Winters Construction** plus its claims taxonomy, operator ordering, and falsification protocol.

# Abstract

Prime numbers exhibit a permanent duality: local irregularity on the number line and global regularity under appropriate asymptotic, algebraic, and spectral coordinates. The Heller–Winters program treats prime detection and prime-structure analysis as a constraint-governed inference problem rather than as either a brute-force search problem or an unconstrained pattern-recognition problem. The construction begins with deterministic admissibility layers—boundedness, wheel residues, parity, symmetric offset shells, figurate constraints, base-independent compartments, and p-adic structure—then passes the surviving state space through quotient normalization, Euler–Mascheroni and Euler–Maclaurin calibration, harmonic signature analysis, and circular/hyperbolic identity-channel decomposition. The resulting residuals are evaluated through policy-governed phase selection, cross-window and cross-base survival, ablation, and an evidence ledger.

The central methodological claim is that any meaningful phase or spectral signal in the primes must survive deterministic constraint removal, multiplicative window changes, base changes, p-adic perturbations, irrational schedule probes, and null-model stress tests. The construction therefore separates hard eliminators from coordinate changes and from explicitly labeled heuristic probes. It is not a license to tune phases until primes appear. It is a discipline for distinguishing invariants from artifacts.

# Claims Ledger

The manuscript uses four claim classes.

**Class A — theorem-backed eliminator or identity.** These are statements inherited from established arithmetic, algebraic, analytic, or combinatorial results. Examples include elementary divisibility implications, wheel admissibility, difference-of-squares factorization identities, Wilson/Fermat/Euler tests when properly stated, and Chebyshev/Bertrand-style boundedness statements.

**Class B — coordinate or normalization convention.** These are transformations or measurement conventions that do not create prime information by themselves. Examples include the log coordinate `u = ln x`, multiplicative windows, quotient normalization, residual definitions, and circular/hyperbolic projections.

**Class C — empirical or reproducibility claim.** These are claims that a construction survives tests: cross-window stability, cross-base stability, p-adic perturbation, ablation, null models, residual-envelope checks, and benchmark comparisons.

**Class D — heuristic or speculative probe.** These include phase gates, ratio schedules, de-aliasing probes, harmonic signatures, or candidate-scoring devices not yet proven as necessary consequences of known theorems. Class D claims are allowed only when labeled and falsifiable.

The manuscript’s governance rule is simple: do not report a Class D observation as a Class A result.

# 1. Preface and Non-Claim Boundary

The Heller–Winters program begins from a practical observation: prime structure is not exhausted by any single representation. Congruence classes expose one kind of order. Factorization identities expose another. Analytic approximations expose another. Zeta-zero oscillations expose still another. Base representations, p-adic neighborhoods, quotient normalizations, and geometric projections all show parts of the object but none should be permitted to impersonate the whole.

The aim of this manuscript is to put those layers into a disciplined order. We do not start with a phase formula and ask it to discover primes. We first remove what arithmetic says is impossible, then normalize what remains, then measure residual structure under adversarial schedule changes. Only after that do phase gates, harmonic signatures, or identity channels become legitimate experimental objects.

The non-claim boundary is important. This manuscript does not assert that primes are generated by metallic ratios, base representations, circular phase gates, or a fixed cosine value. It does not assert that the Riemann Hypothesis follows from the present construction. It does not assert that a next prime is available by closed-form evaluation. It asserts a reproducible framework for organizing constraints and falsifying apparent structure.

# 2. Historical Arc: From Euclid to Riemann

Prime numbers entered mathematics as irreducible arithmetic atoms. Euclid’s infinitude argument established that primes cannot be exhausted by any finite list. That argument is still the model of a structural proof: it does not search for the next prime by enumeration; it changes the state of the problem by showing that finite closure is impossible.

The sieve of Eratosthenes introduced a different primitive: systematic elimination. Instead of identifying primes directly, it removes composites by known divisibility constraints. Modern sieve theory generalizes this principle. The Heller–Winters construction treats sieve logic as a policy layer: a deterministic admissibility filter that defines the feasible domain before any softer inference is allowed.

Euler’s product for the zeta function opened the analytic bridge between primes and continuous objects. Gauss and Legendre clarified the mean-field scale, with the prime number theorem later establishing that prime density is asymptotically governed by the logarithmic law. Riemann then introduced the spectral encoding: prime-counting error is tied to zeros of the zeta function. In log coordinates, those zeros manifest as oscillatory structure.

This history produces the core tension. Arithmetic constraints are discrete, local, and exact. Analytic approximations are continuous, global, and asymptotic. The Heller–Winters program seeks a governed reconciliation: a pipeline in which exact eliminators, coordinate changes, continuous calibrations, and phase analysis each have a defined authority.

# 3. The Heller–Winters Thesis

The thesis is that prime structure should be studied as a constrained, multichannel inference system.

A candidate integer is not first treated as a point to be guessed. It is treated as a state in a policy space. That state is subject to deterministic eliminators, quotient normalizations, geometric projections, schedule perturbations, and evidence-ledger accounting. The surviving structure is then tested for invariance.

The construction has five governing principles.

First, **volume precedes distance**. Prime density is naturally expressed in logarithmic scale. Multiplicative windows are more stable than additive windows when measuring density and residual structure.

Second, **eliminators precede predictors**. Wheel residues, parity, difference-of-squares certificates, and other deterministic filters must be applied before phase or harmonic scoring.

Third, **coordinate changes are not evidence**. A transformation such as `u = ln x`, a quotient normalization, or a circular projection may reveal structure, but it does not by itself prove primality or distributional law.

Fourth, **heuristics must survive hostile schedules**. A signal that only appears under one base, one window length, one phase offset, or one tuned ratio is treated as suspect until it survives cross-base, cross-window, p-adic, and null-model testing.

Fifth, **every claim must enter the ledger**. Prediction events, false positives, false negatives, drift, ablations, residual thresholds, and non-claims are recorded explicitly.

# 4. Reproducibility Contract

The reproducibility contract governs all experiments and all formal claims.

Each experiment must state:

- the integer interval or log-window under study;
- the deterministic eliminators applied;
- the wheel modulus and residue policy;
- the shell and parity rules;
- the coordinate system;
- the normalization denominator;
- the ratio/window schedule;
- the phase gate, if any;
- the null models;
- the expected evidence output;
- the failure criteria.

A claim is not mature until it has been tested under at least four classes of perturbation: scale change, base change, window schedule change, and eliminator ablation. A phase observation that disappears under base change is a base artifact. A quotient observation that disappears under boundary correction may be an endcap artifact. A harmonic signature that disappears under irrational window schedules may be a resonance artifact.

All experiments must distinguish detection, explanation, and proof. Detection says a procedure marks candidates. Explanation says a structure accounts for observed behavior. Proof says the result follows deductively from accepted premises. These are different claims.

# 5. Canonical Coordinates

The canonical coordinate is

```text
u = ln x
```

where `x` is the number-line value and `u` is log-volume. Additive motion in `u` corresponds to multiplicative motion in `x`. A window of width `L` in `u` corresponds to a multiplicative ratio `r = e^L` in value space.

This choice aligns prime-density analysis with the prime number theorem. Locally, prime density behaves like `1 / ln x`, and the average gap behaves like `ln x`. A multiplicative window therefore has a more natural density interpretation than a fixed-width additive interval.

Base change is treated as frequency rescaling, not as a source of arithmetic truth. If `x = b^t`, then `t = ln x / ln b`. Changing base changes the coordinate scale. A true arithmetic invariant should not depend on a special base unless the claim is explicitly about digit structure.

---

# 6. Prime Density Mean Field

The mean field is the leading asymptotic expectation. The simplest local expression is

```text
prime intensity near x ≈ 1 / ln x
```

and the global counting approximation is expressed through `x / ln x` and stronger logarithmic-integral variants. In the Heller–Winters construction, these functions are not treated as final answers. They define a background field against which residuals are measured.

A residual is meaningful only after mean-field structure has been removed with a stable normalization. Otherwise, the model may confuse density drift with signal. This is why quotient functions and Euler–Maclaurin corrections appear later in the pipeline.

The mean field also supplies a complexity baseline. Any candidate-selection method must be compared against the cost of scanning an interval using known primality tests and deterministic sieves. A phase gate that performs worse than wheel-filtered trial division is not useful, even if it looks aesthetically structured.

---

# 7. Boundedness Layer

The boundedness layer supplies hard enclosures. Bertrand–Chebyshev style results ensure primes occur in bounded multiplicative intervals. In the simplest form, for integers above a threshold, there is a prime between `n` and `2n`. This makes the next-prime problem finite over a known interval.

The Heller–Winters use of boundedness is conservative. It does not predict the next prime. It establishes a policy domain in which search or scoring can occur. This is a Class A layer when stated in theorem-backed form.

Boundedness interacts naturally with log windows. The interval `(n, 2n)` corresponds to an interval of width `ln 2` in log space. Other ratios define different windows, but their use must be governed. No ratio schedule is allowed to become a hidden tuning knob.

---

# 8. Wheel Sieve Layer

The wheel sieve removes candidates sharing factors with a chosen modulus `M`, usually a product of small primes. The surviving residue classes modulo `M` form the wheel-admissible state space.

This layer is deterministic. If `gcd(n, M) > 1`, then `n` is composite unless `n` itself is one of the primes dividing `M`. If `gcd(n, M) = 1`, the number is not proven prime; it is merely wheel-admissible.

The wheel is therefore an eliminator, not a predictor. Its output can also be used as a feature space for later analysis, but that secondary use must not be confused with the primary Class A admissibility rule.

Wheel depth has a cost. Larger moduli remove more composites but increase implementation and state complexity. The evidence ledger must record wheel modulus, residue set, and survivor density.

---

# 9. Symmetric Offset Algebra

Symmetric offset algebra begins with the identity

```text
a^2 - b^2 = (a-b)(a+b)
```

This identity produces composite certificates. If an odd integer `n` can be represented as `a^2 - b^2` with nontrivial factors, then it is composite. Conversely, every odd composite has such a representation derived from its factor pair.

The important point is not that difference-of-squares is new. It is that the identity supplies a structured shell geometry around candidate values. Parity constraints, neighbor shells, and generalized offset families can be used to reduce noise and record certificates.

In the Heller–Winters pipeline, shell logic follows wheel logic. Wheel residues remove immediate modular impossibilities; shell certificates organize surviving composites by factor-pair geometry. Shell statistics can later be measured in log windows, but the certificate itself remains deterministic.

---

# 10. Figurate Number Lattices

Figurate numbers supply quadratic and polygonal scaffolds. Triangular numbers, square numbers, and higher polygonal families form structured lattices with predictable second differences.

The role of figurate lattices is not to assert that primes are figurate phenomena. It is to provide coordinate scaffolds that expose local offsets, congruence intersections, and density contrasts. Counting laws around square-root scales may help distinguish arithmetic structure from local sampling noise.

This layer must be handled carefully. Figurate alignments are visually persuasive and therefore dangerous. Every such observation must enter the ledger as a coordinate or heuristic claim unless a theorem-backed eliminator is explicitly present.

---

# 11. Encoding Compartments

Base representations induce digit compartments, carry coupling, and periodic orbit structure. Decimal regularities are not automatically arithmetic invariants. They may be artifacts of base 10.

The construction therefore treats base-`b` compartments as test surfaces. A signal found in base 10 should be tested in base 2, base 3, base 6, base 12, and other governed bases. If the signal is base-specific, it should be reported as such.

Zeckendorf representation adds an aperiodic encoding axis using Fibonacci decomposition. This gives a useful contrast against positional base systems. Cross-base and Zeckendorf survival are de-aliasing tests: they help distinguish arithmetic structure from representation artifacts.

---

# 12. p-adic Structure Layer

The p-adic layer organizes integers by valuations and neighborhoods in divisibility depth. A p-adic ball captures proximity in terms of shared powers of a prime `p`, not ordinary distance.

This layer is useful because many arithmetic constraints are naturally p-adic. Lifting behavior, residue stability, and divisibility depth can be treated as separate from real-line size.

In the construction, p-adic features are usually not eliminators by themselves unless tied to a theorem-backed condition. They are conditioning layers and stress-test axes. A signal that survives real-scale windows but fails under p-adic perturbation may be structurally weaker than it appears.

---

# 13. Quotient Functions and Normalization

Quotient functions compare observed counts or scores with expected mean-field quantities. A raw count is rarely meaningful without a denominator. For example, counts inside windows should be compared against an integral or asymptotic expectation appropriate to that window.

The quotient layer asks:

```text
observed / expected = ?
```

The denominator must be stable, documented, and stress-tested. A bad denominator can create false residuals. Boundary correction and endcap treatment must be stated explicitly.

Quotient functions also support aggregation. If multiple windows, bases, or schedules produce normalized scores, aggregation rules must be fixed before testing. Otherwise, the system becomes an overfit selection machine.

---

# 14. Euler–Mascheroni Calibration

The Euler–Mascheroni constant `γ` appears as the limiting offset between harmonic sums and logarithms:

```text
γ = lim(H_n - ln n)
```

Its role here is conceptual and calibrational. It reminds us that discrete sums and continuous logarithms differ by stable offsets. When a construction mixes counts, logs, and integrals, offsets cannot be ignored.

The manuscript does not treat `γ` as a magical prime key. It is part of the discipline of separating mean field, boundary correction, and residual. Misuse of `γ` as an arbitrary fitted constant is prohibited by the claims ledger.

---

# 15. Euler–Maclaurin Bridge

Euler–Maclaurin summation provides a bridge between sums and integrals. It decomposes a discrete sum into an integral term plus boundary and curvature corrections.

The Heller–Winters construction uses this as a grammar for mean/residual separation. If the observed behavior can be accounted for by a sum-to-integral correction, it should not be reported as a novel prime signal.

This bridge also clarifies window endcaps. In finite intervals, endpoints matter. Boundary terms can dominate small-window residuals. Any phase or harmonic observation that disappears after boundary correction must be downgraded.

---

# 16. Ratio Families and Governance

Ratio schedules define multiplicative windows. The anchors `2` and `10` correspond to octave-like and decade-like log windows. Irrational schedules—metallic ratios, the plastic constant, and the golden ratio—serve as de-aliasing probes.

The purpose is not to claim that primes are generated by these ratios. The purpose is to test whether observed structures survive incompatible window systems. A base artifact will often fracture under irrational windowing.

Ratio governance is necessary. Ratios must be tiered, documented, and limited. A ratio may be added only if its role is specified before evaluation. A ratio may be pruned if it fails stability or contributes only overfit complexity.

---

# 17. Harmonic Signatures

In log coordinates, oscillatory residuals can be represented with sine and cosine components. A harmonic signature is a windowed fingerprint of such residual behavior.

This is where the connection to Riemann-style thinking becomes visible. Zeta zeros contribute oscillatory terms in log scale. However, observing a harmonic pattern is not equivalent to proving a zeta-zero theorem.

Harmonic signatures must be tested for coherence, persistence, base invariance, and null-model survival. A harmonic that appears only after aggressive tuning is a Class D artifact until proven otherwise.

---

# 18. Identity Channels

Identity channels enforce exact algebraic constraints on modeled components. A circular channel satisfies

```text
sin^2 θ + cos^2 θ = 1
```

A hyperbolic channel satisfies

```text
cosh^2 u - sinh^2 u = 1
```

The circular channel organizes phase. The hyperbolic channel organizes envelope growth and decay. Separating them prevents a common error: mistaking envelope drift for phase signal or phase oscillation for growth law.

Off-channel residuals are recorded as mismatch. Identity-channel projection is therefore a denoising operator, but it must not hide failure. If projection discards too much information, the residual ledger should report the loss.

---

# 19. RH-Consistent Envelope Constraint

The Riemann Hypothesis concerns the real parts of nontrivial zeta zeros. In explicit-formula language, the zeros contribute oscillatory terms whose envelope depends on the real part. The critical-line assertion corresponds to an envelope exponent of `1/2`.

The Heller–Winters manuscript treats this as a policy lens, not as a proof. A model may be RH-consistent if its residual-envelope behavior aligns with critical-line scaling. That does not establish RH.

This section’s rule is strict: RH-consistency is a diagnostic label. It is not a theorem claim unless tied to an actual proof.

---

# 20. Phase-Selection Operator

The phase-selection operator, or cosine phase gate, is the most dangerous and therefore most governed part of the construction. It is dangerous because phase gates can be overfit easily.

A legitimate phase gate must declare:

- the input coordinate;
- the window schedule;
- the phase equation;
- the sector quantization;
- all tolerances;
- all exclusions;
- the null models;
- the ablation plan.

The phase gate may score candidates. It may not certify primality. Its value is measured by survival: cross-window, cross-base, cross-residue, p-adic, and null-model performance.

The `90/11` component mentioned in the TOC is treated as a testable normalization component, not as numerology. If it fails ablation, it is removed or downgraded.

# 21. Structural Sieves Beyond the Wheel

Beyond wheel sieves, the construction can combine parity exclusions, shell certificates, figurate constraints, digit compartments, p-adic filters, and quotient-conditioned scoring.

Ordering matters. A cheap deterministic eliminator should run before an expensive heuristic. A certificate-producing layer should be logged before a score-producing layer. Mixed ordering should be benchmarked because interaction effects can create false confidence.

The output of a structural sieve is not only a survivor set. It is also an audit object: which candidates were removed, why, by which theorem or identity, and at what computational cost.

# 22. Optimization View

The construction can be expressed as a constrained optimization problem. Hard constraints define admissibility. Soft scores rank survivors. Regularizers penalize overfitting, schedule fragility, and excess complexity.

The objective is not simply to maximize prime hits. An overfit system can do that locally and fail globally. The objective is to minimize residual mismatch under constraints while preserving generalization across scale, base, p-adic, and schedule perturbations.

A policy-compliant optimization run must output its hyperparameters, constraints, objective terms, failures, and ablation results.

---

# 23. Classical Theorems as Policy Operators

Classical theorems are not decorative citations. They are policy operators.

Euclid provides inexhaustibility posture. Chebyshev/Bertrand provide boundedness. Dirichlet provides progression structure. Wilson provides a primality-equivalent benchmark, though not an efficient practical test. Fermat and Euler provide probabilistic or conditional testing contexts. The Chinese Remainder Theorem provides combiners. Quadratic residues provide structural filters.

Each theorem must be used in its proper authority class. A theorem-backed eliminator can remove candidates. A heuristic influence can guide scoring. The two must not be confused.

# 24. Evidence Ledger and Falsification Protocol

Every prediction or candidate-selection event enters the evidence ledger.

A ledger entry should include:

- candidate value;
- interval/window;
- applied eliminators;
- normalization denominator;
- phase schedule;
- score;
- decision;
- ground truth;
- false-positive/false-negative status;
- residual statistics;
- ablation status;
- drift classification.

The falsification protocol is explicit. A claimed signal fails if it collapses under base change, scale change, schedule change, p-adic perturbation, null-model comparison, or eliminator ablation. A failed signal is not discarded silently; it becomes evidence about the boundary of the construction.

---

# 25. Consolidated Specification

The Heller–Winters construction is the ordered pipeline:

```text
integer interval
  -> boundedness domain
  -> wheel admissibility
  -> parity and residue filters
  -> symmetric shell certificates
  -> figurate / base / p-adic compartments
  -> quotient normalization
  -> Euler–Maclaurin boundary correction
  -> harmonic residual extraction
  -> circular/hyperbolic identity-channel projection
  -> governed phase scoring
  -> evidence-ledger event
  -> ablation and stress testing
```

The governing rule is that later stages may not overwrite earlier authority. A phase score cannot resurrect a theorem-backed composite. A quotient normalization cannot erase a boundary correction failure. A harmonic signature cannot be reported as proof.

The construction’s value is not in any single operator. Its value is in the disciplined composition of operators under a claims ledger.

## Appendix C — Glossary

**Admissibility:** a candidate’s survival under deterministic constraints.  
**Certificate:** a reproducible reason for elimination or classification.  
**Coordinate change:** a transformation used for measurement, not proof.  
**Envelope:** growth/decay constraint on residual magnitude.  
**Identity channel:** projection onto an invariant algebraic manifold.

**Residual:** observed behavior after mean-field and correction terms.  
**Wheel:** residue-class eliminator modulo a product of small primes.

# Closing Statement

The Heller–Winters Theorem, in its current mature form, is best understood as a disciplined construction for prime-structure analysis. It does not ask the reader to accept pattern as proof. It asks that every pattern pass through constraint, normalization, projection, falsification, and ledgered evidence. That is the road from suggestive numerical structure to publishable mathematics.
